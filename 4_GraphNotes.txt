rmsd_plot.png
Root Mean Square Deviation (RMSD) Over Time
This graph tracks the RMSD of the protein backbone throughout the molecular dynamics (MD) simulation, measuring the deviation of atomic positions relative to the initial frame. RMSD provides insights into protein stability, folding behavior, and structural flexibility. It is a key metric for assessing how much the protein structure changes over time and whether it stabilizes into a biologically relevant conformation.

Significance:
RMSD is widely used to evaluate the stability of proteins, protein-ligand complexes, and nucleic acids in MD simulations.
Low RMSD (1-2 Å) suggests that the protein retains a stable conformation, typically seen in well-folded structures.
Increasing RMSD over time may indicate unfolding, domain motion, or large conformational changes.
If RMSD fluctuates around a certain value and does not stabilize, it suggests the system has not reached equilibrium.
Comparing RMSD across multiple simulations or different conditions (e.g., ligand-bound vs. unbound protein) helps identify structural stabilization effects.
Interpretation:
Stable RMSD over time (~1-3 Å): Suggests that the protein structure remains relatively unchanged and has reached equilibrium.
Gradual increase in RMSD: Indicates that the protein undergoes conformational changes, possibly due to unfolding, domain movement, or ligand binding effects.
Fluctuations in RMSD: Can indicate transient conformational states, flexible loops, or allosteric movements.
Large RMSD spikes (>5-6 Å): May suggest a significant event such as ligand-induced conformational changes or partial unfolding.
Generated By:
Software: MDAnalysis
Methodology:
RMSD was calculated using the protein backbone atoms as the reference.
The structure was aligned to the initial frame to remove translational and rotational motion.
The deviation of atomic positions from the reference was computed for each frame.
Visualization:
RMSD values were plotted against simulation time (in frames or picoseconds).



rmsf_plot.png
Root Mean Square Fluctuation (RMSF) Per Residue
This plot measures the per-residue flexibility of the protein throughout the MD simulation by computing the Root Mean Square Fluctuation (RMSF) of each residue’s atomic positions relative to their average structure. RMSF helps identify which parts of the protein are dynamic and which remain rigid.

Significance:
High RMSF values correspond to flexible or disordered regions, such as loops, linkers, or solvent-exposed residues. These regions are often associated with functional movements, such as allosteric sites or flexible binding pockets.
Low RMSF values indicate rigid regions, such as α-helices, β-sheets, and the protein core, which tend to remain stable.
RMSF is useful for identifying functionally important flexible residues that could be involved in ligand binding, signal transduction, or domain rearrangements.
Can help differentiate stable (low RMSF) vs. dynamic (high RMSF) parts of the protein.
Interpretation:
Stable core with flexible loops: A common pattern where the protein's core remains stable (low RMSF), while surface loops and termini exhibit high fluctuations.
Flexible active site residues: If residues near a ligand-binding site have high RMSF, this may suggest conformational changes that allow for ligand accommodation.
Stable secondary structures: If α-helices and β-sheets show low RMSF, it suggests that the protein retains its structural integrity throughout the simulation.
Unexpectedly high RMSF in core regions: May indicate unfolding or significant conformational changes.
Generated By:
Software: MDAnalysis
Methodology:
The RMSF of each residue was computed by measuring its average deviation from its mean position over time.
The entire protein was aligned to the initial structure to ensure only internal fluctuations were captured.
Visualization:
RMSF values were plotted against the residue index.


rg_plot.png
Radius of Gyration (Rg) Over Time
This plot measures the compactness of the protein by calculating the radius of gyration (Rg), which is the root-mean-square distance of all atoms from the protein’s center of mass. Rg is a useful metric for assessing structural stability and folding dynamics.

Significance:
A stable Rg suggests that the protein maintains its overall structure throughout the simulation.
Increasing Rg over time indicates that the protein is expanding, which may occur due to partial unfolding or structural relaxation.
Decreasing Rg may suggest a more compact folding state, collapse, or aggregation.
Rg is useful for detecting large-scale conformational changes, such as domain movements, ligand-induced compaction, or disordered-to-ordered transitions.
Interpretation:
Stable Rg (~constant value): Suggests that the protein maintains its native fold and does not undergo significant structural changes.
Gradual increase in Rg: Indicates unfolding or domain separation, often seen in intrinsically disordered proteins or proteins undergoing conformational change.
Fluctuating Rg: Can indicate partial unfolding/refolding events, which may be important for function.
Ligand-induced compaction: If a protein-ligand complex exhibits a lower Rg than the apo state, this suggests that the ligand stabilizes the protein by inducing a more compact conformation.
Generated By:
Software: MDAnalysis
Methodology:
Rg was calculated by measuring the root-mean-square distance of all protein atoms from the center of mass for each frame in the simulation.
The trajectory was analyzed to exclude rotational and translational motion.
Visualization:
Rg values were plotted over simulation time, with a smooth curve indicating the overall trend.

Spread along PC1 and PC2 axes: If the points spread significantly along these axes, it suggests large-scale conformational changes over time.

Cα atomic positions were extracted from the trajectory.
